Analysis of Police Reported Motor Vehicle Collisions in NYC¶

This website is from a GitHub repository with a deployed Github Pages. The repository with the index.html file can be found at: https://github.com/ben-rishel/MotorCollisionAnalysis

Motor vehicle collisions are a massive problem that plague society. It negatively impacts first responders, drivers, and pedestrians. To make policy decisions to reduce car crashes, it is very important to understand the issue at hand by analzying previous crash data. To get a good sample of crashes, analyzing New York City makes sense because it is the most populated city in the United States. The dataset being analyzed in this notebook is titled "Motor Vehicle Collisions - Crashes" and is from data.gov. It is inclusive of all collisions where either someone is injured, killed, or there is at least $1000 worth of damage in New York City. It has over 10 years of data which comes out to over 2 million rows. This dataset is extremely interesting because it contains the exact day and time of the crash, the number of injuries/deaths of people in the crash and pedestrians, the location, the reason for the crash, and the types of vehicles involved. There are some very interesting questions to investigate in regard to all of this information. This is incredibly important data because reducing car crashes is an important mission for the government and the recognition of patterns in this dataset can inspire policy decisions to reduce crashes. Proper policy changes that reduce car crashes would improve the lives of many first responders, drivers, and pedestrians. It is also important to analyze trends in the data to see if previous policy changes had the desired impact and if they made any effect.

Some of the most pressing questions include:

  1. How common are deaths in NYC motor collisions?
  2. What types of cars are most responsible for crashes?
  3. Is the problem getting worse year-to-year?
  4. What times of the day are most common for crashes?
  5. What times of the year are most common for crashes?
  6. Which boroughs have the most crashes?
  7. Which contributing factors to crashes are most common?
  8. Does the fatality per crash differ for different types of cars?
  9. Does the number of cars in the crash affect the fatality rate?

The bar chart and the last chart present the same information. Our initial question is whether or not the problem of car crashes was getting worse. From this dataset, it is clear that rather than getting worse, it is in fact getting better. The frequency of crashes (or at least those reported) peaked in 2018 and have declined since. The steep decline from 2019-2020/2021 can be attributed to the Covid pandemic, but that does not explain why there have been continually less crashes since. The 2024 data is so low because of the data is incomplete as it only goes up to 4/16/2024. This data is very encouraging but there are several reasons this could not be entirely accurate. For one, many car crashes likely go unreported. Based on the data we have available the problem of car collisions in NYC has been improving in terms of number of crashes. Unexpectedly, the injury charts have less severe of a drop off than the frequency of crash charts. In addition, the number of deaths really have not declined despite the decline in number of crashes. This leads me to believe that the reason for the drop in crash is a reporting issue. It would make sense that less severe crashes do not need to be reported. So, to answer our initial question: There is inconclusive evidence as to whether or not the problem is getting worse. There are significantly less crashes reported, and marginally less injuries, but the death amount is still roughly the same if not worse than it was 5 years ago.
